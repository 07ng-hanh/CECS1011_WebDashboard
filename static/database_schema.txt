# Deciding datatype
- 64-bit integers are designated for timestamp (stored as UNIX timestamp, since such values can be large in the future, and 32-bit timestamp would have a hard limit at year 2038, Jan 19)
- Why not going all in 64-bit integers for other numeric data (IDs and so on?) For such cases, 32-bit integers are more storage-efficient, and we postulate that there wouldn't be a need for tracking more than 2 billion batch codes/user IDs/etc.
16-bit integers can be even more efficient, but risks running out of range sooner (32,767 is the limit max for such numbers)
-> 32-bit ints are the middle ground (2x as space-efficient as 64-bit ints, but still guarantee a safe theoretical limit)

- Why not UUID?
Simply put, UUID takes up more space (it's longer, and made up of characters - not numbers), requires a strategy to avoid duplicate data (we can't just blindly roll a random sequence here - it might repeat itself). Also, sequential INT4 is faster for indexing (due to DB-level optimizations), instead of a bunch of randomly placed UUID strings.
